\section{Project Objective and Current Snapshot}
%==================================================================

\textbf{Objective:} To classify patient functional brain networks into three categories: Cognitively Normal (CN), Alzheimer's Disease (AD), and Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI). The primary challenge is severe class imbalance (very few AD samples compared to CN), necessitating high-quality synthetic data generation.

\textbf{Current Snapshot:}
The engineering foundation of the pipeline (Phases 1 through 5) is now completely built and fully operational. We have introduced rigorous, class-specific quality gates to prevent unrealistic synthetic brains from contaminating the training data. While the pipeline runs reliably from start to finish, the final evaluation metrics remain sensitive to initialisation variance and synthetic data ratios. We have successfully exceeded our baseline Macro-F1 targets in specific configurations, but our immediate focus is on stabilising these gains to ensure AD and MCI recall do not trade off against each other unpredictably.

%==================================================================
\section{Pipeline Overview}
%==================================================================

The workflow operates in five distinct phases:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Phase 1:} Generative model training (learning the topology of healthy and diseased brains).
  \item \textbf{Phase 2:} Guided synthetic generation (creating artificial AD and MCI samples).
  \item \textbf{Phase 3:} Synthetic filtering (discarding noise or exact clones of real patients).
  \item \textbf{Phase 4:} Contrastive pre-training (teaching the model the "language" of brain graphs).
  \item \textbf{Phase 5:} Supervised evaluation (final classification on unseen test data).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Phase 1: Generative Training}
%==================================================================

\subsection{A. Phase Overview}
This phase trains the foundational Generative AI models (Variational Autoencoder and Latent Diffusion Model) to understand and replicate human brain connectivity. It also trains a "Teacher" model that will later guide the diffusion process specifically towards AD or MCI topologies.

\subsection{B. Quality Assessments Added}
We implemented tracking for reconstruction error (how well the model decompresses a brain), latent drift (how stable the mathematical representations are), and Teacher classification accuracy. These checks ensure our foundation models are learning genuine structural patterns rather than memorising the dataset.

\subsection{C. Results and Observations}
\textbf{The Good:} The Autoencoder and Diffusion models show highly stable, healthy reconstruction (VAE MSE $\approx 0.010$) and denoising metrics.
\textbf{The Weak:} Initially, the GCN Teacher model completely collapsed, predicting every patient was AD. This meant it could not provide useful guidance for generating specific diseases.

\centering
\begin{tabular}{@{}lcccc@{}}

\textbf{Teacher Architecture} & \textbf{Macro-F1} & \textbf{CN Recall} & \textbf{AD Recall} & \textbf{MCI Recall} \\
\midrule
Dense GCN (Baseline) & 0.033 & 0.00 & 1.00 & 0.00 \\
Latent MLP (Recovered) & \textbf{0.537} & \textbf{0.62} & \textbf{0.54} & \textbf{0.64} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Teacher model recovery metrics showing the shift from collapse to functional class separability.}
\end{table}

\subsection{D. Changes Made in This Phase}
\textbf{What \& Why:} We overhauled the Teacher model. The initial architecture was failing to differentiate the diseases based on pure topology.
\textbf{Logic:} We switched to a more robust, non-stochastic input representation and implemented strict anti-collapse penalties during training. We also introduced a more capable secondary architecture (Latent MLP) to compare learning capacities.
\textbf{Impact:} This successfully broke the collapse. The Teacher's class-separability score recovered significantly, ensuring it can now reliably guide the creation of realistic AD and MCI synthetic graphs.

\subsection{E. Next Target for This Phase}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Improve Confidence Calibration:} The Teacher's confidence scores are slightly over-optimistic. We will refine its probability scaling so guidance strength is proportional to true certainty.
  \item \textbf{Explore Structural Features:} We will test if explicitly feeding regional brain hubs into the Teacher improves its detection of MCI patterns.
\end{itemize}

\section{Phase 2: Guided Synthetic Generation}
%==================================================================

\subsection{A. Phase Overview}
Using the trained models from Phase 1, we generate thousands of artificial brain graphs starting from pure noise, curving them specifically toward AD or MCI structures using the Teacher's guidance.

\subsection{B. Quality Assessments Added}
We added "Plausibility Gates" that check every generated brain against three criteria: Teacher confidence (does the Teacher believe this is AD?), duplicate risk (is this an accidental exact copy of a real patient?), and spectral plausibility (do the eigenvalues match biological reality?).

\subsection{C. Results and Observations}
\textbf{The Good:} With the new gates, we can aggressively filter out junk generations, retaining up to 40\% of plausible MCI samples.
\textbf{The Weak:} Automatic thresholding can sometimes be too strict. In one test, the gates pruned so many MCI samples that downstream classification failed entirely due to data starvation.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{@{}lcc@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Guidance Settings} & \textbf{AD Retention Rate} & \textbf{MCI Retention Rate} \\
\midrule
Baseline (Strict fixed thresholds) & 0.8\% & 0.0\% \\
Moderate Guidance (Scale=3.0, Auto-$\tau$) & 18.3\% & 40.0\% \\
Aggressive AD Guidance (Scale=8.0, Auto-$\tau$) & \textbf{24.1\%} & \textbf{40.0\%} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Phase 2 generation survival rates showing improvements from decoupled, class-specific guidance limits.}
\end{table}

\subsection{D. Changes Made in This Phase}
\textbf{What \& Why:} We separated the guidance aggressiveness for AD versus MCI and introduced enforceable fallback limits. 
\textbf{Logic:} AD is much harder to generate than MCI because we have fewer real AD samples to learn from. By decoupling the generation controls, we can push the AD generation much harder while being gentler on MCI. We also implemented a safety net: if the quality gates reject too many graphs, the system falls back to a minimum workable quota rather than starving the pipeline.
\textbf{Impact:} This allows precise, class-specific pipeline tuning, though the optimal thresholds still require manual calibration.

\subsection{E. Next Target for This Phase}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Dynamic Thresholding:} Co-calibrate the gating strictness automatically based on the minority class survival rate to prevent data starvation without manual tuning.
\end{itemize}

\section{Phase 3: Synthetic Filtering}
%==================================================================

\subsection{A. Phase Overview}
This phase performs a final, massive cross-correlation check of every synthetic graph against every real graph. It bounds the maximum similarity to ensure generated data is neither noisy garbage nor a privacy-violating clone.

\subsection{B. Quality Assessments Added}
We integrated visual embedding checks (t-SNE clusters) and K-Nearest-Neighbour purity scores to visually and mathematically confirm that synthetic AD brains group together with real AD brains in latent space.

\subsection{C. Results and Observations}
\textbf{The Good:} Retention counts are now perfectly reproducible across runs. The cluster maps show a reasonable expected overlap between real and synthetic data, generating a kNN purity score of 0.462.
\textbf{The Weak:} The mathematical centroid distance between real and artificial MCI patients indicates room for tighter realism.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{@{}lccc@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Filter Bounds} & \textbf{AD Kept} & \textbf{MCI Kept} & \textbf{Probe Macro-F1} \\
\midrule
Strict (0.50 -- 0.98) & 90\% (93/103) & 91\% (152/166) & --- \\
Widened (0.45 -- 0.99) & \textbf{96\%} (99/103) & \textbf{97\%} (161/166) & \textbf{0.4975} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Impact of widened filtering bounds on minority class retention and baseline performance.}
\end{table}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering

    \caption{Phase 3 t-SNE projection visually verifying that the generated synthetic brains structurally overlap and cluster with real patient data.}

\subsection{D. Changes Made in This Phase}
\textbf{What \& Why:} We adjusted the hard correlation bounds to slightly soften the rejection criteria.
\textbf{Logic:} The previous boundaries were discarding highly realistic edge-case patients. By widening them slightly (from 0.50--0.98 to 0.45--0.99), we retain more minority samples while still preventing exact clones.
\textbf{Impact:} Increased retention of crucial AD samples without degrading downstream performance. We have locked a verified "golden" synthetic snapshot to prevent downstream tuning from being confounded by generation variance.

\subsection{E. Next Target for This Phase}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Distance Minimisation:} Fine-tune filtering parameters to specifically pull the mathematical centroid of the synthetic data closer to the true patient data.
\end{itemize}

\section{Phase 4: Contrastive Pre-training}
%==================================================================

\subsection{A. Phase Overview}
Before final classification, we teach our Graph Neural Network to recognise fundamental brain connection patterns using self-supervised learning (without giving it the disease labels yet).

\subsection{B. Quality Assessments Added}
We added epoch-by-epoch tracking of embedding distances and "collapse indicators" to ensure the network is learning to separate different brain structures rather than merging them all together.

\subsection{C. Results and Observations}
\textbf{The Good:} The network learns robust representations without collapsing, maintaining healthy separation between positive and negative graph pairs.
\textbf{The Weak:} Injecting too much synthetic data during this pre-training stage can cause the network to drift away from understanding real biological variations.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{@{}lcccc@{}}

\midrule

\bottomrule

\caption{Downstream impact comparing Real-Only vs. Capped Synthetic regimens during unsupervised pre-training.}
\end{table}

\subsection{D. Changes Made in This Phase}
\textbf{What \& Why:} We introduced explicit caps on how much synthetic data is allowed into the pre-training mix.
\textbf{Logic:} While synthetic data helps class balance, letting it dominate the unsupervised learning phase dilutes the genuine anatomical structures. We capped the synthetic injection explicitly for pre-training independently of the final classifier.
\textbf{Impact:} Capping synthetic data here yielded the best downstream Macro-F1 scores, proving that a balanced curriculum of mostly real brains plus limited synthetic brains works best for representation learning.

\subsection{E. Next Target for This Phase}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Curriculum Tuning:} Determine the exact optimal ratio of real-to-synthetic data strictly for the unsupervised learning phase to maximise minority-class feature extraction.
\end{itemize}

\section{Phase 5: Supervised Evaluation}
%==================================================================

\subsection{A. Phase Overview}
This is the final test. The pre-trained network is attached to a classifier, fine-tuned on the balanced dataset (real + filtered synthetic), and evaluated strictly on a 20\% held-out test set of completely unseen, real patient graphs.

\subsection{B. Quality Assessments Added}
Extensive sweep tracking was built to automatically execute and log dozens of hyperparameter variations (synthetic caps, loss weighting, regularisation) and extract their impact on final AUC, Macro-F1, and class-wise recall.

\subsection{C. Results and Observations}
\textbf{The Good:} We have successfully exceeded the baseline Macro-F1 benchmark (pushing past 0.56 in optimal sweeps) and can achieve excellent AD recall ($>0.60$) under targeted configurations.
\textbf{The Weak:} Results are sensitive. Tuning for high AD recall often degrades MCI recall. Furthermore, identical runs can still fluctuate by several points due to inherent GPU non-determinism, meaning we must rely on average performance across multiple seeds.

\textbf{Impact:} We identified tight operational "bands" where both AD and MCI maintain acceptable recall without catastrophic drops, though run-to-run variance remains an engineering reality.

\subsection{E. Next Target for This Phase}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Variance Quantification:} Shift from evaluating single "best" runs to executing standard 5-seed protocols, reporting the mean and standard deviation for all future experiments.
  \item \textbf{Narrow Grid Search:} Finalise the exact synthetic injection limits that maximise mean Macro-F1 across consecutive runs.

\section{Stability Summary and Execution Plan}
%==================================================================

\subsection{Stability Status}
\textbf{Pipeline Stability (Complete):} The codebase is structurally sound. End-to-end runs execute reliably, quality artifacts generate correctly, and all constraints (e.g., no Focal Loss, no GAT) have been rigorously respected. 

\textbf{Metric Stability (In Progress):} While we can achieve strong peak metrics that exceed historical benchmarks, these peaks are fragile. Slight alterations in random seeds or synthetic counts can collapse minority recall.
